25.1 A Two-Factor ANOVA Model

Key ideas

  • Two factors explain more variation by pairing every level of one factor with every level of the other, so the error term consists only of unexplained noise.
  • Each factor still has its own hypothesis test for main effects after averaging over the other factor.
  • The ANOVA table splits total sum of squares into Distance, Hand, and residual sources, so the smaller error term sharpens inference.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Additive model: yijk = μ + τj + γk + εijk expresses each response as the grand mean μ plus the effect τj of factor A (e.g., Hand), the effect γk of factor B (e.g., Distance), and the random error εijk. All terms share the response’s units (inches from the bull’s-eye).
  • F-ratio: F = MSfactor / MSerror compares the average variation between factor levels (MSfactor) to the average residual variation (MSerror). A large F means the factor causes more shift than random noise.
  • Error standard deviation: σ̂ = √(MSerror) gives the typical spread we expect for any single treatment after removing factor effects.

Short example(s)

  • Dart accuracy: six throws per Hand–Distance treatment yield significant Fs for both factors, so both Hand and Distance affect the mean distance from the bull’s-eye.
  • Paper airplane study: Design (dart/glider × copier/construction paper) and Flaps form two factors with eight treatment groups; the additive model shows which choices shift average flight distance the most.

25.2 Assumptions and Conditions

Key ideas

  • Partial boxplots (residuals plus μ) isolate each factor’s behavior by removing the other factor’s effect.
  • The Additive Enough Condition requires that a factor’s impact stays roughly constant across the levels of the other factor, which we assess via interaction plots.
  • Independence still matters: randomized order or random samples justify the sampling distribution, and residual plots help detect dependence or changing variance.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Partial boxplot construction: subtract the effect of factor B via residuals (yijk − (μ + γk)) and add μ back in, then plot against factor A so you see the variability after holding factor B constant.
  • Additivity check: Parallel lines in an interaction plot of ȳjk mean that ΔA ≈ constant across B and vice versa; significant deviation suggests interaction is needed.

Short example(s)

  • Adjusted Accuracy: Removing Distance reveals the Hand effect more clearly, boosting diagnostic power.
  • Partial boxplots for Distance after removing Hand show tighter spreads and clearer differences among Near, Middle, and Far.

25.3 Adding Interaction to the Model

Key ideas

  • Interaction occurs when changing one factor shifts another factor’s effect, so add an interaction term to the model and test whether it is needed.
  • Fitting interaction consumes (levels A − 1)(levels B − 1) degrees of freedom, so replicating every treatment combo is crucial.
  • When there is a significant interaction, main effects become context-dependent, so focus on interaction plots instead.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Model with interaction: yijk = μ + τj + γk + (τγ)jk + εijk. The extra term (τγ)jk captures how the combination of level j and level k shifts the response beyond the additive effects.
  • Interaction degrees of freedom: (levelsA − 1)(levelsB − 1) is the number of independent interaction parameters, which come out of the residual df.
  • Residual definition: εijk = yijk − (μ̂ + τ̂j + γ̂k + (τγ̂)jk) now measures error after main effects and interaction are removed.

Short example(s)

  • Dart accuracy with interaction: The interaction term is significant (F ≈ 8.52, p ≈ 0.0012), and the residual plot becomes well behaved once the interaction is in the model.
  • Leaded fuel plot: Crossing lines in the interaction plot (Additive × Car Type) show that the additive’s effect depends entirely on the engine design, so only the interaction plot tells the full story.
