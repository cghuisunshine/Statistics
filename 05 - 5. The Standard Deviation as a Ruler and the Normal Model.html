The Standard Deviation as a Ruler and the Normal Model – Chapter 5 Tutorial

5.1 Standardizing with z-Scores

Key ideas

  • z-scores measure distance from the mean in standard deviation units, enabling fair comparisons across variables and units.
  • Positive z means above the mean; negative means below; larger |z| = more unusual.
  • Standardizing does not change distribution shape; it just recenters and rescales.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • z-score: \(z = (y - \bar{y}) / s\) or \(z = (y - \mu) / \sigma\). Meaning: how many SDs a value is from its mean; unitless ruler.

Short example(s)

  • Heptathlon: Chernova’s long jump z ≈ +1.1; Ennis’s 200 m z ≈ -2.1 (faster is better), so Ennis’s run is more exceptional.
  • Bode Miller (Vancouver 2010): Slalom z ≈ -1.03, downhill z ≈ -1.23; downhill was his stronger relative performance.

5.2 Shifting and Scaling

Key ideas

  • Adding/subtracting a constant shifts all position measures by that constant; shape and spread stay the same.
  • Multiplying/dividing by a constant rescales all position and spread measures by that constant (variance by constant²); shape unchanged.
  • Unit changes (kg → lb, °C → °F) are rescaling + shifting; interpret summaries accordingly.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Shift: \(y' = y + c\). Means/medians/percentiles shift by c; IQR/sd unchanged.
  • Rescale: \(y' = ky\). Means/medians/percentiles multiply by k; IQR/sd multiply by k; variance multiplies by \(k^2\).

Short example(s)

  • NHANES male weights: Subtracting 74 kg centers on “kg above recommended” but spread same; multiplying by 2.2 to pounds multiplies mean, quartiles, IQR, and sd by 2.2.
  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: Linear transform shifts and rescales; shape identical, sd scales by 9/5.

5.3 Density Curves and the Normal Model

Key ideas

  • Density curves model distributions with total area 1; areas under the curve approximate proportions of data.
  • The Normal model \(N(\mu,\sigma)\) is bell-shaped, symmetric; parameters μ and σ set centre and spread.
  • Standard Normal \(N(0,1)\) underlies z-scores; use only when Nearly Normal Condition holds (unimodal, symmetric).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Normal density: \(f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}\). Meaning: smooth model with area = 1; shape controlled by μ, σ.
  • Standardize for model: \(z=(y-\mu)/\sigma\). Meaning: places any Normal value on the standard Normal scale.
  • 68–95–99.7 Rule: For Normal models, ~68% within 1σ, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ of μ. Meaning: quick probability/percentile estimates.

Short example(s)

  • Newborn weights: Density curve fitted to histogram; area between 2450–2750 g gives proportion of babies in that range.
  • Clara Hughes (5000 m speed skating): Time just over 1σ faster than mean → expect ~2–3 racers faster (top ~16%), consistent with bronze finish.
  • TOEFL 600: z = 1.0 → about 84th percentile by 68–95–99.7 Rule.

5.4 Finding Normal Percentiles

Key ideas

  • For any Normal model, convert to z then use technology or tables to find the percentile (area below z).
  • Areas between two values are differences of cumulative areas; sketches help avoid sign mistakes.
  • Chebyshev’s Rule is a distribution-free bound (≥ 1 − 1/k² within k sd) but looser than Normal percentages.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Percentile lookup: Given z, find \(P(Z \le z)\) from table/technology. Meaning: proportion of a Normal population at or below that value.
  • Chebyshev: At least \(1 - 1/k^2\) within k sd (any distribution). Meaning: conservative bound when Normality is doubtful.

Short example(s)

  • TOEFL 648: z = 1.80 → percentile ≈ 0.964; only ~3.6% score higher.
  • EPA mpg: Use Normal with μ=24.8, σ=6.2 to find percent above 31 mpg (z ≈ 1.0 → ~16%) or between 31–37.2 (between z=1.0 and z=2.0 → ~13.5%).

5.5 Normal Probability Plots

Key ideas

  • Normal probability plots (NPPs) check Normality: plot ordered data vs. expected Normal scores; straight ≈ Normal.
  • Systematic curvature indicates departures: S-shape for heavy tails, concave/convex for skew.
  • NPPs often reveal non-Normality more clearly than histograms; always pair with a distribution plot for context.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Normal scores: Expected z-values for ordered data under N(0,1). Meaning: benchmark to compare sample quantiles to Normal quantiles.

Short example(s)

  • Nissan mpg: NPP nearly straight with minor low-end deviation; Normal model reasonable.
  • NHANES weights: Histogram right-skewed; NPP curved upward in upper tail → Normal model poor.
