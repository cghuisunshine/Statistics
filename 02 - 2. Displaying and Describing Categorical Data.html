Displaying and Describing Categorical Data – Chapter 2 Tutorial

2.1 Summarizing and Displaying a Single Categorical Variable

Key ideas

  • Follow the three rules: make a picture, make a picture, make a picture—visuals reveal patterns, surprises, and outliers that tables hide.
  • Use the area principle: graphical area should be proportional to the values represented; distorted areas mislead.
  • Frequency tables (counts) and relative frequency tables (percents) describe how cases fall into categories.
  • Bar charts (counts or percents) and pie charts (parts of a whole) are standard displays; choose based on clarity of comparisons.
  • Check the categorical data condition: data must be counts/percents of non-overlapping categories before using bar or pie charts.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Frequency table: Lists each category with its count. Meaning: shows how many cases fall in each group.
  • Relative frequency table: Lists each category with its proportion/percent of the total. Meaning: standardizes counts for fair comparison.
  • Area principle: Graphical area ∝ data value. Meaning: prevents visual exaggeration (e.g., oversized icons or 3D pies).
  • Bar chart / relative bar chart: Equal-width bars whose height shows count or percent. Meaning: easy visual comparison across categories.
  • Pie chart: Circle sliced with angles/areas proportional to category percents. Meaning: shows part-to-whole; harder for fine comparisons.

Short example(s)

  • Titanic ticket class: Frequency table (First 325, Second 285, Third 706, Crew 885) becomes a bar chart obeying the area principle; reveals most aboard were not crew and Third > First or Second.
  • Heavy drinking by region: Thematic map categorizes percent heavy drinkers; categorizing a quantitative measure can clarify regional patterns.

2.2 Exploring the Relationship Between Two Categorical Variables

Key ideas

  • Contingency tables cross-classify cases by two variables; margins give individual (marginal) distributions.
  • Joint distribution = cell percent of the grand total; marginal distribution = totals for one variable; conditional distribution = distribution of one variable within a category of the other.
  • Comparing conditional distributions across categories shows association; if they match, variables are independent.
  • Visuals: side-by-side or stacked (segmented) bar charts of conditional percents; drop one category when binary to simplify.
  • Simpson’s paradox: overall percents can reverse the story once data are broken into meaningful groups—always ask “percent of what?”

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Contingency table: Grid of counts for all category combinations. Meaning: foundation for two-variable categorical analysis.
  • Marginal distribution: Totals (counts or percents) for one variable, found in table margins. Meaning: how cases distribute on one variable ignoring the other.
  • Joint distribution: Each cell’s percent of the overall total. Meaning: probability a random case falls in a specific category pairing.
  • Conditional distribution: Distribution of one variable restricted to a category of the other (row or column percents). Meaning: answers “within this group, how do outcomes split?”
  • Independent variables: Conditional distributions of one variable are the same across all categories of the other. Meaning: knowing one gives no info about the other.
  • Segmented bar chart: Bars scaled to 100% and divided by conditional percents. Meaning: quick visual check for equality of conditional distributions.
  • Simpson’s paradox: Trend/advantage seen overall reverses within groups when a lurking variable is considered. Meaning: avoid combining groups that differ on a confounding factor.

Short example(s)

  • Titanic survival by class: Conditional survival percents: First 62.5%, Second 41.4%, Third 25.2%, Crew 24.0%. Survival and class are associated (not independent).
  • Living arrangements by sex (young adults): Conditional percents show women more often in couples, men more often living with parents—visualized with side-by-side bars.
  • Fish intake vs. prostate cancer: Conditional cancer risk ranges ~7–11%; small “never/seldom” group hints at higher risk but sample is small—illustrates cautious interpretation.
  • Simpson’s paradox (pilots/day-night): Usha beats Pierre in day and night separately, but Pierre’s overall rate looks higher because he flew more day flights (easier conditions).
