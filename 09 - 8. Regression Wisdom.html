Regression Wisdom – Chapter 8 Tutorial

8.1 Examining Residuals

Key ideas

  • Regression isn’t finished until residuals are checked; they reveal nonlinearity, non-constant spread, and subgroups.
  • Residual plot should show no pattern if the Straight-Enough and Equal-Variance conditions hold.
  • Multiple modes in residuals or histograms can flag distinct groups (lurking categorical variables).

Short example(s)

  • Penguin dive heart rate vs. duration: Scatterplot looked roughly linear, but residuals showed a clear bend—linearity violated.
  • Breakfast cereals: Residual histogram showed side modes; grouping by “healthy” vs. plain cereals explained high/low residual clusters.

8.2 Unusual Observations: Leverage & Influence

Key ideas

  • High leverage: x-value far from x-mean; can pull the fitted line.
  • Influential: removing the point changes slope/intercept noticeably; requires both leverage and a discordant y.
  • Outliers (large residual) need not be influential if x is ordinary; high-leverage points with small residuals can still anchor the line.

Short example(s)

  • Armspan vs. height (CensusAtSchool): An unusually tall/short student has high leverage; check influence by refitting without them.
  • Planet distances: Outer planets have extreme x; a mismeasured outer point would strongly influence the fitted line.

8.3 Extrapolation & Subsets

Key ideas

  • Never trust predictions far beyond observed x-range; linear trends can change (piecewise or nonlinear reality).
  • Hidden subgroups (e.g., primates vs. carnivores; cereal shelf levels) can yield different slopes; fit separately when appropriate.
  • Piecewise models may be better when trends shift over time (e.g., immigration percentages by decade).

Short example(s)

  • Asian immigration % over time: Different slopes before/after policy shifts; extrapolating to 1955 gave poor prediction.
  • Cereal shelves: Separate lines by kid/eye-level shelves revealed different calorie–sugar relationships.

8.4 Correlation ≠ Causation & Lurking Variables

Key ideas

  • Strong linear association does not prove causality; lurking variables may drive both x and y.
  • Subgroup differences (gender, species type, shelf placement) can masquerade as or mask relationships.
  • Report context and subject-matter rationale before implying cause.

Short example(s)

  • Cereal “healthiness”: Higher fat inflates calories; sugar alone doesn’t cause calories—other nutrients lurk.
  • Armspan/height by gender: Separate gender lines differ; gender is a lurking variable if ignored.

8.5 Averages, Restricted Range, and Re-expression

Key ideas

  • Averaging can inflate correlation (ecological correlations); restricted ranges can deflate correlation/slope.
  • Re-expressing (logs/square roots) can linearize curved relationships before fitting a line.
  • Always recheck residuals after transformation; choose the model that straightens patterns and stabilizes spread.

Short example(s)

  • Brain weight vs. gestation: Log or sqrt of brain weight straightens the curve; separate fits for primates vs. carnivores improve modeling.
  • Immigration counts: Piecewise or transformed trends better than a single linear extrapolation.
