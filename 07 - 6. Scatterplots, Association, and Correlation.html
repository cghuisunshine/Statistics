Scatterplots, Association, and Correlation – Chapter 6 Tutorial

6.1 Scatterplots

Key ideas

  • Scatterplots show the relationship between two quantitative variables; plot one point per case (x = explanatory, y = response).
  • Describe: direction (positive/negative/none), form (linear/curved/none), strength (tight vs. diffuse), and unusual features (outliers, clusters).
  • Add a categorical variable via color/symbols to reveal subgroups (lurking variables).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • No formula—focus on visual description. Positive association: as x increases, y tends to increase; negative association: as x increases, y tends to decrease.

Short example(s)

  • Brain weight vs. gestation: Positive, curved pattern; separating primates vs. carnivores reveals two distinct curves and a lurking categorical variable.
  • Worldwide prices (iPod/Big Mac): Plot shows currency/purchasing power differences; strong positive association, potential outliers.

6.2 Correlation

Key ideas

  • Correlation (r) measures strength/direction of a linear association between two quantitative variables.
  • Unitless, between –1 and +1; sign = direction; magnitude = strength (closer to |1| = stronger linear fit).
  • Correlation is sensitive to outliers and only captures linear trends; not resistant; does not imply causation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Correlation: \(r = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum \left(\frac{x_i-\bar{x}}{s_x}\right)\left(\frac{y_i-\bar{y}}{s_y}\right)\). Meaning: average product of paired z-scores; measures how jointly high/low the standardized values are.

Short example(s)

  • Ventilation cost vs. speed: Linear scatter with strong positive r (example inference of strength/direction).
  • Study time vs. exam score: Positive r expected; outlier of a long study but low score would weaken r.

6.3 Straightening Scatterplots (optional)

Key ideas

  • When form is curved, re-express one or both variables (logs, square roots, reciprocals) to achieve a straighter pattern.
  • Straightening clarifies direction/strength and makes correlation more informative.
  • Choose transforms guided by shape: right-curved increasing patterns often benefit from log(y), log(x), or both.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • No single formula; common re-expressions: \(y'=\log(y)\), \(x'=\log(x)\), \(y'=\sqrt{y}\), \(y'=1/y\). Meaning: compresses large values, stabilizes spread, linearizes multiplicative relationships.

Short example(s)

  • Brain weight vs. gestation: Taking logs (not shown in text example, but hinted) would likely straighten the curved positive trend.
  • GDP per capita vs. HDI: Raw GDP is right-skewed; log(GDP) vs. HDI yields a tighter, more linear association.

Conditions & Cautions

Key ideas

  • Quantitative Variables Condition: Both variables must be quantitative for scatterplots/correlation.
  • Straight Enough Condition: Correlation summarizes linear association; check scatterplot for linear form before using r.
  • Outlier Condition: Outliers can distort r; identify and report with/without when necessary.
  • Correlation ≠ causation: Association does not prove cause; lurking/confounding variables may drive observed patterns.

Short example(s)

  • Lurking variable: In brain weight vs. gestation, species type (primate/carnivore) is a lurking categorical variable that changes the pattern.
  • Outlier impact: A single extreme point in a linear cloud can drag r toward –1 or +1 or mask a true trend.
