Experiments and Observational Studies – Chapter 10 Tutorial

10.1 Observational Studies

Key ideas

  • Observational studies record choices/outcomes without assigning treatments; cannot establish causation because of lurking variables.
  • Retrospective: look back at existing data/records; Prospective: follow subjects forward in time.
  • Useful for rare outcomes or when experiments are impractical/unethical; prone to confounding and limited control.

Short example(s)

  • Music students vs. grades: Retrospective comparison shows higher grades for music students, but differences could be due to preexisting habits/support, not music itself.
  • Pet kidney failure investigation: Retrospective study of affected animals’ diets/exposures to identify possible causes.

10.2 Randomized, Comparative Experiments

Key ideas

  • Experiments deliberately assign treatments to subjects (experimental units) to test cause/effect.
  • Principles: Control what you can, Randomize the rest, Replicate (enough units), Block when feasible.
  • Factor: explanatory variable; Levels: specific values of a factor; Treatment: combination of factor levels.
  • Random assignment balances lurking variables across groups, enabling causal inference.

Short example(s)

  • Sleep/exercise on grades: Randomly assign students to 4/6/8 hrs sleep × 0/30 min exercise; compare grade outcomes.
  • Contrast bath study: Randomized, controlled comparison of contrast baths +/– exercise vs. exercise alone to assess swelling reduction.

10.3 Blinding and Control

Key ideas

  • Blinding reduces bias: subjects, evaluators (or both) unaware of treatment assignments (single vs. double-blind).
  • Control group provides a baseline (placebo or standard treatment) to compare active treatments.
  • Placebos mimic treatment experience to isolate treatment effect from expectation effects.

Short example(s)

  • Vitamin E post-surgery: Use placebo ointment vs. vitamin E ointment with blinded patients and assessors to measure healing speed.
  • Shingles ointment trial: Randomize 8 patients to new vs. standard ointment; blinding limited but placebo-like control helps reduce bias.

10.4 Blocking and Factorial Designs

Key ideas

  • Block on known nuisance variables (e.g., gender, severity) to reduce variability and isolate treatment effects within homogeneous groups.
  • Factorial designs vary multiple factors simultaneously to study main effects and interactions efficiently.
  • Randomize within blocks; keep treatment allocation balanced across blocks.

Short example(s)

  • Frumpies cereal preference: If Saturday-cartoon viewers might respond differently, block by viewing status when testing old vs. new flavour.
  • Two pesticides in corn plots: Apply both pesticides across many plots; randomize within blocks (e.g., field sections) to compare effectiveness.

10.5 Confounding & Causal Claims

Key ideas

  • Confounding: when effects of a factor and another variable are intertwined; observational studies especially vulnerable.
  • Only well-designed randomized experiments justify causal conclusions; observational studies suggest associations/hypotheses.
  • Statistical significance ≠ causation; consider design, control, and potential confounders before claiming cause.

Short example(s)

  • Wine and health: Observational studies show wine drinkers healthier, but wealth/education lurk; cannot claim wine causes better health.
  • Weekend hospital deaths: Observational finding of higher weekend mortality; possible confounders include staffing levels, case severity.
