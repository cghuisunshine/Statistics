Sample Surveys – Chapter 9 Tutorial

9.1 Three Big Ideas of Sampling

Key ideas

  • Examine a part of the whole: use a sample to learn about a population.
  • Randomize: random selection protects against unknown influences and bias.
  • Sample size matters (not population size/fraction): accuracy depends mostly on n, not what fraction of the population is sampled.
  • Bias is fatal: biased methods (convenience, voluntary, poor frames) over/under-represent groups and can’t be fixed after the fact.

Short example(s)

  • Ipsos Reid history poll: Conclusion about all Canadians comes from ~1000 adults because random sampling + adequate n yields representativeness.
  • Literary Digest 1936: Phone/car-owner frame during Depression undercovered poorer voters → biased prediction for Landon.

9.2 Simple Random Samples (SRS)

Key ideas

  • Every subset of size n has equal chance; use random numbers/technology to select.
  • Sampling frame: the list from which you draw; quality of frame affects coverage.

Short example(s)

  • Customer database SRS of 8000: Two independent SRSs matched well on age, income, wealth—evidence of representativeness via randomization.

9.3 Other Sampling Designs

Key ideas

  • Stratified sample: Divide into homogeneous strata, SRS within each → improves precision for subgroup comparisons.
  • Cluster sample: Randomly select heterogeneous clusters (e.g., schools), survey everyone in clusters → efficiency when lists are hard.
  • Systematic sample: Random start, then every k-th unit; simple to implement with ordered lists.
  • Multistage: Combine methods (e.g., stratify → cluster → sample within).

Short example(s)

  • 3 randomly chosen churches → members within: Cluster sample (churches are clusters); may miss variation if clusters not representative.
  • Call every 10th cafeteria customer: Systematic sample; risk of bias if flow is periodic or venue under-represents non-users.

9.4 Sources of Bias

Key ideas

  • Voluntary response: People choose to participate; tends to attract strong opinions.
  • Convenience sample: Easy-to-reach subjects; often unrepresentative.
  • Undercoverage: Parts of population not in frame (e.g., no phone).
  • Nonresponse: Selected individuals can’t/won’t respond; may differ systematically.
  • Response/wording bias: Question wording or interviewer influence skews answers.

Short example(s)

  • Online Ephedra poll: Voluntary response of site visitors → not generalizable despite large n.
  • Park playground survey of park visitors: Convenience; over-represents park users/parents with kids.
  • Wording: Leading wording about “too small playground” biases responses toward repair/expansion.

9.5 Practical Sampling Tips

Key ideas

  • Define population & frame clearly; anticipate who might be missed.
  • Use random selection; adjust contact strategy (call-backs, time variation) to reduce nonresponse.
  • Match design to logistics: stratify when subgroups matter; cluster for cost; systematic for ordered lists.
  • Large n cannot fix bias; focus on design first.

Short example(s)

  • Phone surveys midday only: Undercover workers; improve by varying call times and using RDD (random digit dialing) to include unlisted/cell numbers.
  • Employee satisfaction: Stratify by role (managers/foremen/labourers) to ensure representation of each group.
