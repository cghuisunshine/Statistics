From Randomness to Probability – Chapter 11 Tutorial

11.1 Random Phenomena

Key ideas

  • A random phenomenon has known possible outcomes but we cannot predict the result of a single trial.
  • Each repetition is a trial with an outcome; outcomes combine into events, and all outcomes together form the sample space S.
  • Long-run relative frequency stabilizes; the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) says that with independent, identically distributed trials, the relative frequency approaches a single value.
  • Empirical probability describes that long-run proportion; independence means past results do not change the chance of the next outcome.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • P(A) = ( # times A occurs ) / ( total # of trials )
    Long-run relative frequency definition of probability; counts of event A divided by all trials, yielding a proportion between 0 and 1.
  • Law of Large Numbers: relative frequency of A → P(A) as trials → large (with stable probabilities and independent trials)
    Guarantees that repeating the same random process many times causes the running proportion to settle near the true probability.

Short example(s)

  • Observing the Portage and Main light each day shows the “green” proportion settling near 0.35; more days smooth the wiggles.
  • Flipping six heads in a row does not make a tail “due”—independent flips keep the chance of heads at 0.5 each time.

11.2 Modelling Probability

Key ideas

  • Theoretical probability comes from a model (often equally likely outcomes) instead of observation.
  • When outcomes are equally likely, probability depends on counting outcomes in the event versus outcomes in the entire sample space.
  • Not all events are equally likely; personal or subjective probabilities express belief rather than long-run frequency.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • P(A) = ( # outcomes in A ) / ( # possible equally likely outcomes )
    For balanced models (fair coins, fair dice), probability is a simple fraction of favourable outcomes over all possible outcomes.

Short example(s)

  • Two coin flips have sample space S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}; event “one head and one tail” has 2 outcomes, so P = 2/4 = 0.5.
  • From a well-shuffled deck, P(face card) = 12/52 = 3/13; P(ace of spades) = 1/52.
  • Subjective probability: a student’s “I’m 70% sure I’ll get an A” reflects belief, not a long-run repeatable process.

11.3 Formal Probability Rules

Key ideas

  • Probabilities are bounded: 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1; the total across the entire sample space is 1.
  • The complement of A (written AC) contains all outcomes not in A.
  • Disjoint (mutually exclusive) events have no outcomes in common; for them we can add probabilities directly.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
    Probability is always a proportion; negative chances or values above 1 are impossible.
  • P(S) = 1
    The probabilities of all possible outcomes together must exhaust 1 (total certainty).
  • P(AC) = 1 - P(A)
    The chance of “not A” is everything left after accounting for A.
  • If A and B are disjoint, P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
    For outcomes that cannot happen together, add their probabilities to get the chance of either one.

Short example(s)

  • Traffic light: if P(green) = 0.35 and P(yellow) = 0.04, then P(red) = 1 - (0.35 + 0.04) = 0.61 by complementing the other colours.
  • Checking a distribution: if probabilities assigned to student years sum to 0.90, some outcomes or probability mass are missing because totals must equal 1.

11.4 The General Addition Rule

Key ideas

  • When events overlap, adding their probabilities double-counts the intersection; subtract that overlap to correct.
  • “A or B” in probability is inclusive (A, B, or both); “A or B but not both” removes the intersection.
  • Venn diagrams help visualize overlap, complements, and exclusive parts of events.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
    General addition rule: total chance of either event equals their sum minus the probability of both (to avoid double-counting shared outcomes).
  • P(A or B but not both) = P(A) + P(B) - 2P(A and B)
    Exclusive “or”: remove the overlap entirely by subtracting it twice.

Short example(s)

  • Intersection stop: P(stop for red) = 0.65, P(stop for bus) = 0.16, P(both) = 0.03 ⇒ P(stop) = 0.65 + 0.16 − 0.03 = 0.78.
  • Campus survey: P(relationship) = 0.33, P(sports) = 0.25, P(both) = 0.11 ⇒ P(relationship or sports) = 0.33 + 0.25 − 0.11 = 0.47; P(either but not both) = 0.36.

Quick recap

  • Probability as long-run proportion links empirical data to theoretical models; LLN justifies interpreting stabilized relative frequencies as probabilities.
  • Legitimate assignments must stay between 0 and 1 and sum to 1 across the sample space.
  • Use complements and addition rules to simplify calculations, switching to the general addition rule when events can occur together.
