Displaying and Summarizing Quantitative Data – Chapter 3 Tutorial

3.1 Displays for Quantitative Variables

Key ideas

  • Use the Quantitative Data Condition: values must be numerical with known units before drawing histograms, stem-and-leaf plots, or dotplots.
  • Histograms slice values into equal-width bins; bar height shows count (or percent). Bin width choice can reveal or hide features.
  • Relative frequency histograms rescale the y-axis to percents; shape stays the same.
  • Stem-and-leaf plots show individual values while revealing shape; good for small to moderate samples.
  • Dotplots place one dot per case; great for small data sets and spotting clusters or gaps.
  • Gaps signal missing ranges; spikes may hint at rounding or heaping; always follow the area principle.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Histogram: Divide the quantitative axis into equal bins; count cases per bin. Meaning: visualizes the distribution’s shape and density.
  • Relative frequency: Percent in bin = (bin count / total) × 100. Meaning: standardizes counts for fair comparison.
  • Stem-and-leaf: Split each value into stem (leading digit[s]) and leaf (next digit). Meaning: preserves raw values while showing distribution.
  • Dotplot: One dot per case along a scale. Meaning: shows individual values, clusters, and gaps directly.

Short example(s)

  • Tsunami magnitudes: Histogram with 0.5-unit bins shows most quakes near 7; finer 0.2-unit bins reveal a spike at 7.0 (likely rounding) and a gap from 4.2–4.4.
  • Pulse rates (24 women): Stem-and-leaf exposes that all values are multiples of 4, implying the nurse counted 15 seconds then ×4.
  • Kentucky Derby times: Dotplot shows two clusters; the race distance changed in 1896, explaining the gap.

3.2 Shape

Key ideas

  • Describe shape with modes (unimodal, bimodal, multimodal, uniform), symmetry vs. skew (left/right), and tail length.
  • Look for outliers and gaps; they can signal distinct groups, errors, or important cases.
  • Always discuss shape before centre and spread; shape guides which summaries are appropriate.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Mode (graphical): Peaks of the histogram/dotplot. Meaning: common value regions; multiple modes may indicate mixed groups.
  • Skewness: Longer tail direction defines skew (right = positive, left = negative). Meaning: shows imbalance; often suggests using median/IQR.
  • Outlier (rule-of-thumb): Values far from the main body; later formalized with 1.5 IQR fences in boxplots. Meaning: potential errors or interesting cases.

Short example(s)

  • Credit card spending: Unimodal, strongly right-skewed with a few negatives; shape warns against using mean alone.
  • Earthquake magnitudes: Unimodal, slight left tail; outliers at 9.0–9.1 (Japan/Sumatra) and a few small quakes.
  • Housing people per unit: Histogram with three low outliers prompts investigation of those cities.

3.3 Centre (Median)

Key ideas

  • The median splits ordered data so half the values are below and half above; units match the data.
  • Use the median for skewed data or when outliers are present; it is resistant to extreme values.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Median position (odd n): (n + 1) / 2. Meaning: locates the middle value in the sorted list.
  • Median (even n): Average of the two middle values. Meaning: balances half the data on each side.

Short example(s)

  • Tsunami quakes (1989–2013): n = 221; median at position 111 = 7.2 magnitude, describing a “typical” tsunami-causing quake.
  • Blue Jays wins: Median season wins is the midpoint of the ordered win counts since 1993; half the seasons are above, half below.

3.4 Spread (Range & IQR)

Key ideas

  • Spread shows how far values vary around the centre; always report a spread with any centre.
  • Range uses extremes; very sensitive to outliers. IQR focuses on the middle 50% and is resistant.
  • Quartiles are the 25th (Q1) and 75th (Q3) percentiles; median is the 50th percentile.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Range: max − min. Meaning: total span of the data; influenced by extremes.
  • IQR: Q3 − Q1. Meaning: width of the middle half; pairs naturally with the median.
  • Percentile: Value below which a given percent of data fall. Meaning: locates positions in ordered data.

Short example(s)

  • Tsunami quakes: Q1 = 6.7, Q3 = 7.6 → IQR = 0.9 magnitude units; middle half is tightly clustered.
  • Divorce rates (Canada): IQR captures how much rates vary year-to-year without being swayed by single highs/lows.

3.5 Boxplots & 5-Number Summary

Key ideas

  • 5-number summary: min, Q1, median, Q3, max; concise snapshot of centre and spread.
  • Boxplot: box from Q1 to Q3 with median line; whiskers to most extreme values within fences.
  • Fences flag possible outliers: values beyond 1.5 × IQR from Q1 or Q3 are plotted separately.
  • Median position and whisker lengths show skewness; boxplots excel at comparing groups.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Fences: Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5×IQR; Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5×IQR. Meaning: rule-of-thumb boundaries for suspect outliers.
  • Whiskers: Extend to most extreme values inside fences. Meaning: show main data range without outliers.

Short example(s)

  • Tsunami magnitudes: 5-number summary: 4.0, 6.7, 7.2, 7.6, 9.1. Fences ≈ 5.35 and 8.95 flag the 9.0–9.1 quakes and tiny 4.0 quake as outliers.
  • Fuel economy boxplot: Electric outlier (112 mpg equivalent) appears beyond whisker; IQR shows typical subcompact mpg spread.

3.6 Mean, Variance, and Standard Deviation

Key ideas

  • Mean (average) balances the data; sensitive to outliers and skew; best for roughly symmetric distributions.
  • Variance and standard deviation (sd) measure typical squared/unsquared deviation from the mean; sd shares data units.
  • Always pair mean with sd; they summarize centre and spread together.
  • Empirical Rule (for mound-shaped data): ~68% within 1 sd, ~95% within 2 sd, ~99.7% within 3 sd of the mean.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Mean: \bar{y} = \sum y_i / n. Meaning: balance point; total divided by count; units match data.
  • Deviation: y_i − \bar{y}. Meaning: how far each value is from the mean (can be negative/positive).
  • Variance (sample): s² = Σ(y − \bar{y})² / (n − 1). Meaning: average squared deviation; uses n−1 to avoid underestimating spread.
  • Standard deviation: s = √(Σ(y − \bar{y})² / (n − 1)). Meaning: typical distance from the mean in original units.

Short example(s)

  • Tsunami quakes (n=221): Mean ≈ 7.15, sd ≈ 0.70; Empirical Rule → ~95% of magnitudes between 5.75 and 8.55.
  • Credit card spending: Mean $478.19, sd $741.87, but skew/outliers make median/IQR more trustworthy.
  • Nissan fuel efficiency: Mean 22.4 mpg, sd 2.45; symmetric shape justifies mean/sd; outlier noted but mild impact.

3.7 Telling the Story

Key ideas

  • Always report shape, centre, spread, and unusual features together; match summaries to shape.
  • Skewed or outlier-prone data → median, IQR, 5-number summary (optionally note mean/sd with caution).
  • Roughly symmetric, unimodal data → mean and sd (optionally median/IQR); IQR often slightly larger than sd for symmetric data.
  • Avoid over-precision; one or two decimals beyond data resolution is enough.
  • Never use histograms for categorical data; never use bar-chart language (shape/centre/spread) for categories.

Short example(s)

  • Flight cancellations: Histogram skewed right with an extreme month >20% cancelled; median/IQR summarize typical months better than mean/sd.
  • Fuel economy (Maxima): Unimodal, near-symmetric; mean 22.4 mpg, sd 2.45 mpg describe expected variation; low outlier flagged.
