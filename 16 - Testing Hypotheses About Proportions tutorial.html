Testing Hypotheses About Proportions (Chapter 16)

16.1 Hypotheses

Key ideas

  • The null hypothesis (H₀) is the skeptical default—“nothing has changed”—and always sets a specific parameter value like H₀: p = 0.20.
  • The alternative hypothesis (Hₐ) lists the parameter values we consider believable if the null is disproven; it can look for increases, decreases, or any difference.
  • We compare observed proportions to the null model using a standard deviation “ruler” built from the hypothesized value.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • H₀: parameter = hypothesized value specifies exactly what claim is being tested, so the sampling model is fully defined.
  • Hₐ chooses the direction(s) of interest and decides which tail(s) of the distribution contain evidence.
  • SD(p̂) = sqrt(p₀·q₀ / n) (with q₀ = 1 - p₀) gives the spread of sample proportions when the null is true; because p is a proportion, the units remain in the 0–1 range.

Short example(s)

The ingot plant assumes H₀: p = 0.20 and hopes for a drop. With n = 400, the null gives SD(p̂) = 0.02, so any observed p̂ can be compared to that variability.

16.2 P-Values

Key ideas

  • A P-value is the probability of observing the sample result (or something even more extreme) if the null hypothesis is true; it quantifies “reasonable doubt.”
  • Small P-values (e.g., < 0.05) signal surprising data that cast doubt on the null; large P-values mean we fail to reject.
  • Think of the P-value as a conditional probability that allows us to weigh evidence objectively.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • z = (p̂ - p₀) / SD(p̂) counts how many standard deviations the sample proportion is away from the null; because the denominator is in proportion units, z itself has no units.
  • One-sided P-value: P(Z ≤ z) or P(Z ≥ z), depending on the direction. Two-sided P-value: 2·P(Z ≥ |z|). Each formula turns the z-score into a probability via the standard Normal.

Short example(s)

With p̂ = 0.17, p₀ = 0.20, and SD = 0.02, the z-score is -1.5. The one-sided P-value is P(Z ≤ -1.5) ≈ 0.067, so only 6.7% of samples would produce such a low rate if the true cracking rate were still 20%.

16.3 The Reasoning of Hypothesis Testing

Key ideas

  • Follow the four steps: Hypotheses, Model, Mechanics, Conclusion.
  • Check the conditions: randomization/independence, success/failure counts with n·p₀ ≥ 10 and n·q₀ ≥ 10, and the 10% rule.
  • The one-proportion z-test uses the Normal model determined by the null; it is the foundation of testing proportions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • n·p₀ ≥ 10 and n·q₀ ≥ 10 ensure there are enough expected successes and failures so that the Normal approximation is valid; they count observations, so the unit is “cases.”
  • z = (p̂ - p₀) / sqrt(p₀·q₀ / n) is the test statistic that leads to the P-value; the denominator comes from the null model, so the statistic measures surprise under that assumption.

Short example(s)

In the Ministry of Transportation survey (n = 90, x = 61), the sample proportion is 0.678, the null is p₀ = 0.80, the standard deviation is 0.042, and z ≈ -2.90. The P-value is 0.002, so the evidence is strong enough to reject the null.

16.4 Alternative Alternatives

Key ideas

  • Choose the appropriate alternative before looking at the data: two-sided (p ≠ p₀) if any change matters, one-sided (p < p₀ or p > p₀) if only one direction matters.
  • One-sided tests halve the two-sided P-value when the data move in the hypothesized direction, but you must justify the choice in advance.
  • Never decide the alternative after seeing the data—decide on the “Why” first.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • P-value (two-sided) = 2·P(Z ≥ |z|) reflects the combined chance of witnessing equally extreme deviations in both tails.
  • P-value (one-sided) = P(Z ≤ z) or P(Z ≥ z) matches the direction of the alternative; it only uses the relevant tail.

Short example(s)

The Punjab hospital study had p̂ = 0.569 versus p₀ = 0.517, giving z = 2.44. For a two-sided question, the P-value is 2·P(Z ≥ 2.44) ≈ 0.0146, which strongly contradicts the null that the proportion equals 51.7%.

16.5 P-Values and Decisions

Key ideas

  • The threshold for rejecting the null varies by context: for high-stakes claims (e.g., airplane safety) we demand smaller P-values than for exploratory questions.
  • Always report the P-value alongside your conclusion so others can make their own decisions about the evidence.
  • After a significant test, follow up with a confidence interval to understand how big the effect might be; this links statistical significance to practical importance.
  • Stick to the plan: don’t define hypotheses or alternatives after seeing the data, and remember that “fail to reject” is not the same as “accept.”

Important formulas & their meaning

  • SE(p̂) = sqrt(p̂·q̂ / n) estimates how much the sample proportion wiggles when the true proportion is unknown; its units are proportions.
  • ME = z* × SE(p̂) gives the margin of error for a confidence interval; z* is the critical value (e.g., 1.96 for 95%), so the interval is p̂ ± ME.

Short example(s)

Following the Punjab test, SE = 0.0211 and ME = 1.96×0.0211 ≈ 0.041, so the 95% confidence interval is (0.528, 0.610). This interval shows how large the true proportion of male births might be, supplementing the P-value of 0.0146.

16.6 Large Sample Tests of Hypotheses

Key ideas

  • For other parameters (e.g., means) with large samples (n ≥ 60), the sampling distribution of the estimator is approximately Normal, so we can form z-tests just like we do with proportions.
  • Check independence, avoid extreme skewness or outliers, and ensure the sample is representative before trusting the Normal approximation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • z = (estimate - hypothesized value) / SD(estimate) generalizes the test statistic: the numerator is the gap between observation and claim, and the denominator is the estimator’s standard deviation under the null.
  • For a mean, SD(ȳ) = s / sqrt(n) and we replace the unknown population SD with the sample SD s, giving SE(ȳ) = s / sqrt(n). The units remain the same as the original measurement (e.g., ppm).

Short example(s)

Testing salmon for mirex, the sample mean is 0.0913 ppm, s = 0.0495 ppm, and SE = 0.0495/√150 ≈ 0.00404 ppm. The z-score is (0.0913 - 0.08)/0.00404 ≈ 2.80, so P(Z ≥ 2.80) ≈ 0.0026, giving strong evidence that the true mean exceeds the safety threshold.
