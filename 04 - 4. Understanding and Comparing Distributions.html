Understanding and Comparing Distributions – Chapter 4 Tutorial

4.1 Comparing Groups

Key ideas

  • Comparisons matter: ask how a quantitative variable differs across groups defined by a categorical variable.
  • Use consistent scales when comparing histograms or stems across groups to avoid visual distortion.
  • Summaries (median, IQR, mean, sd) should be reported for each group; choose median/IQR for skew, mean/sd for roughly symmetric shapes.
  • Back-to-back stem-and-leaf plots are handy for small samples to compare two groups on one axis.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • 5-number summary (per group): min, Q1, median, Q3, max. Meaning: frames centre and spread, enabling quick group contrasts.
  • IQR (per group): Q3 − Q1. Meaning: width of the middle 50%—compare variability between groups.
  • Mean & sd (per group): \(\bar{x} = \sum x / n\); \(s = \sqrt{\sum (x-\bar{x})^2 / (n-1)}\). Meaning: typical level and typical deviation when shapes are not severely skewed.

Short example(s)

  • Wind speed: Summer vs. Winter: Winter days have higher median wind (~1.72 mph) and larger IQR (1.82) than Summer (median ~0.71 mph, IQR 1.27); winter histogram is flatter, summer more skewed right.
  • Crime rates by region: Back-to-back stems show Western CMAs have higher motor vehicle theft and homicide rates; Thunder Bay is an Ontario outlier in homicide.
  • Mug insulation: Boxplots of temperature loss after 30 minutes show the Nissan mug with smallest median loss (best at keeping heat) and tightest IQR; SIGG loses heat fastest.

4.2 Comparing Boxplots

Key ideas

  • Side-by-side boxplots efficiently compare centres, spreads, and potential outliers across many groups (e.g., months).
  • Ordering boxplots naturally (time, dose, etc.) reveals patterns and seasonality.
  • Outliers are context-dependent: a value ordinary in one group may be extreme in another.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Box boundaries: Lower edge = Q1; upper edge = Q3; line = median. Meaning: shows the central 50% and typical middle.
  • Whiskers & fences: Whiskers reach most extreme inlier within 1.5×IQR of Q1/Q3; values beyond fences flagged as suspect outliers. Meaning: visual rule-of-thumb for unusual points.

Short example(s)

  • Monthly wind speeds: Boxplots show winds strongest and most variable Oct–Apr; calm, tight spreads Jun–Aug. New outliers emerge within-month (e.g., a rare windy August day).
  • Roller coaster speeds: Median speeds similar for wood and steel, but steel has larger spread and more outliers (including the extreme Formula Rossa).

4.3 Outliers

Key ideas

  • Outliers merit attention: they may be errors, key events, or part of a long tail; investigate before deciding.
  • Never drop outliers silently; report analyses with/without them when necessary.
  • Outlier status depends on context (grouping, time); a value can be ordinary globally but extreme within a subgroup.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Fence rule: Values < Q1 − 1.5×IQR or > Q3 + 1.5×IQR are flagged. Meaning: heuristic to spot potentially unusual cases.

Short example(s)

  • Wind outliers: Windiest February day = winter storm; June outlier = rare Massachusetts tornado day; August outlier = Hurricane Irene. Context explains extremes.
  • Formula Rossa coaster: Extreme speed traced to hydraulic launch system—distinct mechanism vs. gravity-driven coasters.
  • Data error caution: 1950 census “14-year-old widowers” traced to column shifts on punch cards—illustrates why to check anomalies.

4.4 Timeplots

Key ideas

  • Order matters: plot quantitative values against time to spot trends, cycles, and changes in variability.
  • Smoothers (moving averages, lowess, median smoothers) reveal underlying trends by reducing point-to-point noise.
  • Be cautious forecasting; physical/seasonal patterns may continue, but economic/social series may not.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Moving average (concept): At time t, average values in a window around t. Meaning: local typical level; window width controls smoothness.

Short example(s)

  • Daily wind speeds: Timeplot shows calm, low-variance summer and higher, volatile winter; lowess smooth highlights seasonal dip and rise.
  • Roller coaster track lengths: Smoothed timeplot suggests tracks shortening since ~1970s, though long outliers remain.
  • Airline load factors: Timeplots with smoothers show rising trend and seasonal cycles; different smoothers (lowess vs. median) can highlight broad vs. local patterns.

4.5 Re-expressing Data

Key ideas

  • Re-expression (transformations) can tame skew, stabilize variance, and make group spreads comparable.
  • Right-skewed, positive data often benefit from log, square root, or reciprocal; left-skewed may benefit from squaring or exponentials.
  • After re-expression, reassess shape, centre, spread, and outliers; interpretation shifts to the transformed scale.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Log transform: \(y' = \log_{10}(y)\) or \(\ln(y)\) for \(y>0\). Meaning: compresses large values, often symmetrizing right-skewed data; differences in logs correspond to ratios on the original scale.
  • Square root: \(y' = \sqrt{y}\) for \(y \ge 0\). Meaning: moderates right skew, especially for count-like data.
  • Reciprocal: \(y' = 1/y\). Meaning: flattens high values; useful when large original values correspond to small effects (e.g., times, rates).

Short example(s)

  • CEO pay (2012): Raw compensation is extremely right-skewed; log10 pay is roughly symmetric with centre ~6.84 (≈ $6.9M). Outliers remain identifiable but clearer.
  • Cotinine levels: Raw scales hide ETS vs. nonsmokers because smokers’ spread dominates; log transform makes spreads comparable and shows ETS exposure raises cotinine above unexposed nonsmokers.
  • Assets histogram: Heavy skew can be clarified via square root or log; log scale turns multiplicative differences into additive, aiding comparison.
