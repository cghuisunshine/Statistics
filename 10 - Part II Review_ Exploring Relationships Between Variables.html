Part II Review – Exploring Relationships Between Variables

Quick Review (Ch. 6–8)

  • Scatterplots: Plot quantitative y vs. x; describe direction, form, strength, and unusual features; add group symbols for categorical subgroups.
  • Correlation (r): Measures strength/direction of linear association (–1 to +1); unitless; sensitive to outliers; only for quantitative variables and linear form.
  • Regression line: Least squares line minimizes sum of squared residuals; ŷ = b0 + b1x; slope in y-units per x-unit; residual = y – ŷ.
  • Goodness of fit: R² = fraction of variability in y explained by x; residual SD (s) = typical prediction error.
  • Diagnostics: Residual plots should show no pattern; look for curvature, unequal spread, outliers, leverage/influential points.
  • Cautions: Don’t extrapolate far beyond data; association ≠ causation; hidden subgroups and range restrictions affect r and slopes.
  • Re-expression: Logs/roots/reciprocals can straighten curves and stabilize spread; refit and recheck residuals afterward.

Strategy Reminders

  • Check conditions: Quantitative variables, Straight-Enough, Outlier/Influence check, and for inference later, Normal-ish residuals and equal variance.
  • Always pair slope interpretation with units; intercept may lack practical meaning if x = 0 is outside data range.
  • Use R² to convey explained variation; use residual plots to validate the form and spot subgroups.
  • When groups differ, consider separate models or include the categorical variable (with different symbols/lines).
