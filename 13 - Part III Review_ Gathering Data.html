Part III Review – Gathering Data

Quick Review (Ch. 9–10)

  • Sampling for populations: Use random samples (SRS, stratified, cluster, systematic) to estimate population parameters; avoid bias (voluntary response, convenience, undercoverage, nonresponse, wording).
  • Sample size matters: Accuracy depends on n, not on population fraction; randomization protects against unknown influences.
  • Observational studies: Retrospective vs. prospective; can identify associations but cannot establish causation due to lurking/confounding variables.
  • Experiments for causation: Randomly assign treatments; principles—Control, Randomize, Replicate, Block when helpful; factors/levels/treatments clearly defined.
  • Blinding & control groups: Placebos/standard treatments + single/double blinding reduce bias; use control groups for baseline comparison.
  • Blocking & factorial designs: Block on known sources of variation; vary multiple factors to study main effects/interactions.
  • Cautions: Bias can’t be fixed by large n; observational studies don’t prove causation; ensure ethical/practical design.

Strategy Reminders

  • Define population, frame, and variables; choose sampling method that ensures coverage and randomness; plan contact/call-backs to reduce nonresponse.
  • For experiments, specify factors/levels/treatments, response variables, random assignment method, blinding, control, replication, and blocking if needed.
  • Interpret “statistically significant” as evidence unlikely by chance under the model, not as practical importance or proof of cause unless design supports causation.
