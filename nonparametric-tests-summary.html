Chapter 28 Nonparametric Tests

This summary captures the main concepts, formulas, and examples for each section of Chapter 28.

28.1 Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test

Key ideas

  • Replace observations with pooled ranks and compare rank sums instead of raw values.
  • The null hypothesis assumes identical distributions; the alternative seeks a systematic shift.
  • Use the rank sum for the smaller sample and evaluate its extremity via exact tables or a Normal approximation.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Total ranks: 1+2+…+n = n(n+1)/2 – This is the fixed pool of rank points; once one sample’s rank sum is known, the other follows.
  • E(Ti) = ni(n1+n2+1)/2 – The average rank sum expected for each group under the null, reflecting the group size.
  • Var(Ti) = n1n2(n1+n2+1)/12 – Spread of the rank-sum sampling distribution; key for the Normal approximation.
  • z = (Ti − E(Ti))/√Var(Ti) – Standardizes the observed sum for approximate P-values when table entries are unavailable.

Example

Biology majors had T1=42 and Psychology majors T2=24; expected E(T2)=30. The rank sum of 24 is not extreme enough (exact tail probability >0.05), so we do not reject the null.

28.2 Kruskal-Wallis Test

Key ideas

  • Generalizes Wilcoxon to more than two independent groups by ranking all observations together.
  • Rank sums are adjusted for group sizes, and we assess how spread out they are.
  • The sampling distribution approximates a chi-square with degrees of freedom equal to the number of groups minus one.

Important formula

  • H = (12/(N(N+1)))·∑(Ti2/ni) − 3(N+1) – Measures variation among group rank sums. Larger values signal systematic differences among treatments.

Example

Hand-washing methods produced rank sums 46.5, 142.5, 154.5, and 184.5 with total N=32. Plugging into the formula gives H=15.17, and the upper tail of χ²3 gives P=0.002, so washing method matters.

28.3 Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test

Key ideas

  • Used for paired or blocked designs where differences within pairs are ranked.
  • Tie-handled by omitting zeros; the test statistic is the smaller of the positive and negative rank sums.
  • The null is no systematic shift; the alternative is a consistent higher or lower tendency for one condition.

Important formulas

  • T+ + T− = n(n+1)/2 – Total rank points from the nonzero differences.
  • E(T+) = E(T−) = n(n+1)/4 – Expected signed rank sum under the null.
  • Var(T±) = n(n+1)(2n+1)/24 – Variability used in the Normal approximation.
  • z = (T^* − E)/√Var – Standardizes the observed smaller sum for large n.

Example

Wine tasters produced a smaller sum T=1.5 out of n=8 nonzero differences. Tables give critical value 3 at α=0.05, so P<0.05 and we reject equivalence in favor of a wine effect.

28.4 Friedman Test

Key ideas

  • Treatments are ranked within blocks to eliminate block effects before comparing treatments.
  • The test statistic gauges how far treatment rank sums deviate from equality.
  • Large-sample reference is the chi-square distribution with t−1 degrees of freedom.

Important formula

  • S = (12/(bt(t+1)))·∑Ti2 − 3b(t+1) – Treatment rank sums determine the spread; b is number of blocks, t number of treatments.

Example

With three litters (blocks) and three diets (treatments), rank sums were 6, 9, and 3, giving S=6.0. The χ²2 upper tail yields P=0.05, so diet likely affects weight gain.

28.5 Rank Correlation

Key ideas

  • Spearman’s correlation measures monotonic (not necessarily linear) association by correlating ranks instead of raw values.
  • Less sensitive to nonlinearity and outliers, and it accepts ordinal data.
  • Inference only requires independence of the paired ranks plus random sampling.

Important formula

  • rs = 1 − (6∑di2)/(n(n² − 1)) – Differences between ranks \(d_i\) fold directly into the correlation; values range from −1 to +1.
  • t = r_s√(n−2)/√(1−r_s²) – Use when \(n ≥ 10\) to test \(H_0: r_s = 0\) with a \(t_{n-2}\) distribution.

Example

Blue Jays had Pearson \(r=0.18\) but Spearman \(r_s=0.52\). The larger \(r_s\) reflects the strong monotonic increase in salary with home runs even though the relation is not strictly linear, and the t-test gives \(P≈0.08\).
