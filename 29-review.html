Quick Review

Key ideas

  • Inference about relationships lets us move from sample facts (crawling ages, birth weights, reading scores) to conclusions about how variables behave in the world.
  • Confidence intervals estimate plausible ranges for parameters, while hypothesis tests assess the plausibility of an assumed model.
  • Normal (z), Student’s t, chi-square, and regression models each serve different types of questions; all rely on checking the right assumptions.
  • Especially in regression, inspect scatterplots and residual plots before trusting the straight-line form.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Confidence interval: estimate ± z*SE (or t* when σ is unknown). The interval lists all parameter values consistent with the data; its units match the quantity being estimated (weeks, grams, percentage points). For example, the CI for the difference in birth weights between First Nations and Chinese-origin babies uses the two means, the pooled SE (√(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)), and a t critical value to give a plausible range in grams.
  • Hypothesis test statistic: (estimate – null value)/SE. The resulting z or t measures how many SEs the sample result lies from the null. In the perfect-pitch study (7% vs. 32%), the null of equal proportions gives a large z (≈), a minuscule P-value (~0.0001), and thus strong evidence against the null.
  • Central Limit Theorem (CLT): For large samples, the sampling distribution of proportions or means is approximately Normal. This justifies using Normal-based methods for the preterm twin rates (20/43 vs. 26/48) once each success and failure count exceeds 10.
  • Student’s t distribution: When σ is unknown, replace it with the sample SD and use t with df = n–1 (or technology-provided df). The paired maze experiment uses a t model on differences; the mean difference ± t*SE captures how the scent affects completion times while acknowledging the extra uncertainty from estimating SD.

Short example(s)

  • Crawling age comparison: January vs. July baby means differ by −3.8 weeks. A two-sample t-test divides that difference by the unpooled SE to yield a t statistic; the accompanying CI highlights the range of weeks where the true difference likely falls.

Assumptions for Inference

Key ideas

  • Independence is required for every procedure; random sampling or allocation ensures it, while sampling more than 10% without replacement makes the result conservative.
  • Proportion procedures need at least 10 successes and 10 failures; t procedures need roughly Normal populations or residuals; chi-square tests require every expected count ≥ 5.
  • Paired data (e.g., before/after repairs) must be treated via a one-sample t on the differences, not as independent samples.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Success-failure rule: Ensure np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10 before using a Normal model for proportions. For the twin preterm example, both 20 and 23 exceed 10, so the Normal approximation holds.
  • Chi-square expected counts: expected = (row total × column total)/grand total, and every expected count must be ≥ 5. The genetics class’s 1:3:3:9 ratio yields expected counts well above 5, so the chi-square goodness-of-fit test is valid.
  • Normality for t/regression: Inspect histograms or Normal probability plots (or residual histograms). The pottery aluminum oxide study and the tableware regression both reported approximately Normal residuals, justifying the t or regression inference.

Short example(s)

  • BC birth weights: Before comparing the two ethnic groups, confirm independence (each newborn is distinct), sufficient sample size, and that the weight histograms are fairly symmetric so the two-sample t procedure is trustworthy.

Quick Guide to Inference

Key ideas

  • Ask “one sample or two?”, “quantitative or categorical?”, “paired or independent?” to choose the right model (z, t, χ², regression).
  • The guide organizes procedures for proportions (z intervals/tests), means (t intervals/tests), categorical distributions (χ² models), and linear regression (t intervals/tests for slopes and prediction intervals for values).
  • Technology typically supplies degrees of freedom for two-sample t’s, and plotting residuals helps validate linear regression assumptions.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • 1-Proportion SE: √[p(1−p)/n]. When estimating the proportion of preterm twins in 1990 (20/43), this SE quantifies how much the sample proportion might bounce around.
  • 2-Proportion SE: √[p₁(1−p₁)/n₁ + p₂(1−p₂)/n₂]. In the perfect-pitch study, it scales the observed 25-point difference before converting to a z statistic and P-value.
  • 2-Sample Mean SE (unpooled): √[s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂]. The Ashley Rails vs. New Forest aluminum oxide means use this SE to build a 95% CI for the difference in percentages.
  • Paired t SE: s_d/√n where s_d is the SD of differences. The maze experiment transforms each subject’s scented-minus-unscented time into one difference and uses this SE to test whether scent matters.
  • Chi-square statistic: Σ (observed − expected)² / expected. Comparing the observed tongue-rolling counts to the expected 1:3:3:9 distribution reveals whether the genetics model fits.
  • Regression slope t: t = b₁ / SE(b₁), with SE(b₁) = s / √Σ(xᵢ − x̄)². The tableware regression reports b₁ ≈ 2.49 dollars per minute and SE = 0.1416, so t ≈ 17.6, producing a precise CI for the slope (dollars per minute of polishing).

Short example(s)

  • Regression prediction: Given the fitted line between cottage cheese and ice cream sales, plug in 82 million pounds of cottage cheese to predict ice cream sales and build a 95% prediction interval that accounts for both slope uncertainty and residual variability.

Review Exercises

Key ideas

  • These mixed problems require numbering the right inference procedure, checking its assumptions, and interpreting the CI or P-value in context.
  • Covers two-sample means, paired t, proportion comparisons, chi-square (goodness-of-fit, homogeneity, independence), and regression, so listen for cues like “paired,” “counts,” or “associated with.”
  • Translate conclusions back to the research narrative (e.g., “Does the scent improve maze performance?” or “Is ALS more common among athletes?”).

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Margin of Error (ME): critical value × SE. For the burglary loss CI of ($1644, $2391), ME = ($2391 − $1644)/2 = $373.50, so we’re ±$373.50 around the point estimate.
  • P-value interpretation: Probability of getting a statistic as extreme as the one observed if the null is true. In the cracker–bloat chi-square, a small P-value suggests the type of cracker is linked to bloating severity.
  • Type I vs. Type II error: If the regression recommendation to feed bass natural food turns out to be wrong despite a low P-value, we’d have committed a Type I error; failing to detect a real effect would be Type II.

Short example(s)

  • Genetics goodness-of-fit: With 122 students and a 1:3:3:9 expectation, compute expected counts (≈6.8, 20.3, 20.3, 54.6), plug the observed counts into the χ² formula, and interpret the resulting P-value to judge the genetic theory.
  • Tableware regression: The slope of 2.49 (dollars/minute) with SE 0.1416 yields a 95% CI of roughly 2.21 to 2.77. That tells us that each extra minute of polishing is associated with about a $2.49 increase in retail price, with high confidence that the true slope lies within that range.
