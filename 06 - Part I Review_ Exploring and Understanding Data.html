Part I Review – Exploring and Understanding Data

Quick Review of Core Ideas

  • Categorical vs. quantitative: Choose displays and summaries that match the variable type.
  • Categorical tools: Frequency/relative frequency tables; bar and pie/segmented bar charts; side-by-side comparisons; conditional distributions for associations.
  • Quantitative tools: Histograms, boxplots, stem-and-leaf, dotplots; describe shape (modes, symmetry/skew), centre, spread, and unusual features (gaps/outliers).
  • Summaries: 5-number summary (min, Q1, median, Q3, max) and IQR for resistant centre/spread; mean and sd for unimodal symmetric data; always pair centre with spread.
  • Transforms: Re-express skewed-right data with logs/square roots/reciprocals to improve symmetry or equalize spreads across groups.
  • Standard deviation as ruler: z-scores compare values across scales; shifting shifts position only; rescaling multiplies position and spread.
  • Normal model: Use when Nearly Normal (unimodal, symmetric); 68–95–99.7 Rule gives quick area estimates; NPP/histogram check fit.

Worked Reminders

  • Comparing groups: Use consistent scales; boxplots side-by-side for many groups (months, regions); conditional distributions for categorical vs. categorical.
  • Outliers: Investigate, correct if error; report analyses with/without if impactful; context can change outlier status.
  • Timeplots: Plot in order; smoothers (moving average/lowess) reveal trend/seasonality; beware of over-forecasting.
  • Percentiles in Normal: Standardize then use table/tech; sketch to avoid sign mistakes; Chebyshev gives conservative bounds if not Normal.

High-Level Strategy for Exercises

  • Identify W’s (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How); classify variables; choose displays accordingly.
  • For skewed quantitative data: median + IQR; consider log/sqrt re-expression if heavily skewed.
  • For symmetric unimodal data: mean + sd; 68–95–99.7 Rule for quick probabilities.
  • Compare groups with aligned scales; boxplots for many groups, histograms/dotplots/stems for small samples.
  • When modelling Normal: verify Nearly Normal with histogram/NPP; standardize to z for percentile questions.
