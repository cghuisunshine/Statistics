Chapter 22 Comparing Counts

22.1 Goodness-of-Fit

Key ideas

  • Compare the observed counts of a single categorical variable with a hypothesized distribution (e.g., uniform zodiac signs for CEOs).
  • Expected counts come from the model; deviations are aggregated into a χ² statistic.
  • Conditions: counts only, independent responses, and expected counts ≥ 5. The χ² curve is skewed, and the notion of “large” depends on the degrees of freedom.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Expected count = N × hypothesized proportion — N is the total number of observations and the proportion comes from the null model; the product is the count you would expect under that model.
  • χ² = Σ (Obs − Exp)² / Exp — sum of squared deviations between observed and expected counts, each scaled by the expected count, so large values signal poor fit.
  • Degrees of freedom = k − 1 — with k categories, only k − 1 residuals are free because totals constrain the last cell; df determine which χ² curve gives the P-value.

Short example(s)

  • Canadian NHL birthdays: expected January births = 1184 × 0.08 = 94.72, yet 127 were observed. Summing all 12 cells gives χ² = 54.09 with df = 11, P < 0.005, so uniform birth distribution is rejected.

22.2 Chi-Square Test of Homogeneity

Key ideas

  • Determine whether one categorical variable has the same distribution across several populations (e.g., post-graduation plans for four colleges).
  • Expected counts use pooled proportions; calculate the same χ² statistic as in goodness-of-fit.
  • Conditions remain counts, independence within and between groups, and expected counts ≥ 5.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Expected count = (row total × column total) / grand total — compute the marginal totals, multiply row and column totals, and divide by the overall total to get the number expected.
  • χ² = Σ (Obs − Exp)² / Exp — sums the same scaled residuals as before, but now over every cell of the contingency table.
  • Degrees of freedom = (R − 1)(C − 1) — R is # of response categories (rows), C is # of groups (columns); df counts how many cells’ differences vary independently.

Short example(s)

  • Graduating classes: χ² = 93.66 with df = 6 yields P < 0.0001, so post-graduation plans are not homogeneous across the four colleges.

22.3 Examining the Residuals

Key ideas

  • After rejecting a χ² null, inspect individual cells to see what drives the rejection.
  • Standardized residuals rescale each cell’s (Obs − Exp) to account for its expected count.
  • Large positive residuals show more observations than expected; large negatives show fewer.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Standardized residual = (Obs − Exp) / √Exp — scaling the raw difference produces a z-score; sign indicates whether a cell has surplus or deficit counts.

Short example(s)

  • Engineering graduates are much more likely to go to grad school (+3.50) and much less likely to take “Other” choices (−4.45) than expected.
  • Hockey players: January (+3.32) and February (+2.74) show surpluses while August (−2.66) and December (−2.54) show deficits.

22.4 Chi-Square Test of Independence

Key ideas

  • Test whether two categorical variables measured on the same individuals are independent (e.g., tattoo status vs. hepatitis C).
  • Mechanics match the homogeneity test, but the question is now about association between variables.
  • Mind the same conditions plus caution about small expected counts; combine categories when needed.

Important formulas & their meaning

  • Expected count = (row total × column total) / grand total — under independence, the cell count equals the product of marginal totals scaled to the grand total.
  • χ² = Σ (Obs − Exp)² / Exp and df = (R − 1)(C − 1) — identical to homogeneity because the contingency table structure is the same.
  • Standardized residual = (Obs − Exp) / √Exp — reused to highlight individual cell contributions and flag tiny expected counts.

Short example(s)

  • Hepatitis C study: χ² = 57.91 with df = 2 gives P < 0.0001, so tattoo and hepatitis status are not independent. The parlour-tattoo cell has a huge positive residual; combining tattoo rows still rejects independence (χ² = 42.42).
